## background — content — The performance of Global Health Partnerships is increasingly important and scrutinized, yet achieving high performance is proving challenging
**Layout:** Two framed content boxes side by side, each with a light steel-blue header band; square (▪) bullets with en-dash sub-bullets. Vertical rule / gutter between.
**Column 1 — Increasing role of GHP performance:**
- GHPs play a major role in global health
- Performance of GHPs can have huge impact on the health of the world's population
- The focus on performance is increasing, driven by
  - Increasing donor focus on impact, effectiveness, and efficiency
  - Increasing number of Partnerships in global health
  - Likelihood of lower funding growth or less funding, given the financial crisis
**Column 2 — Challenging factors:**
- Complex environment and nature of GHP organizations create challenges, e.g.
- **Objective-setting:** distinguishing between the change a GHP hopes to bring about in the world vs. the goals it sets itself that will help bring about the change
- **Accountability:** ensuring accountability and delivery in the context of loose Partnership structures, voluntary membership, and limited hierarchy
- **Capabilities:** gaining the capacity and capabilities needed to continuously improve performance
_Source: McKinsey analysis._

## goals — content — The project will deliver practical insights on improving GHP performance based on piloted improvement ideas
**Layout:** Three framed content boxes side by side (Goals / Approach / 3 main end-products), each with a light steel-blue header band and square bullets; even gutters.
**Column 1 — Goals:**
- Develop a joint perspective, tested and proven, on how GHPs can improve performance, by
  - Exploring how to improve performance in a GHP, not simply adopting existing (e.g., private-sector) approaches
  - Testing new ways of working with Stop TB bodies that could lead to higher performance
  - Developing a joint perspective to share with the global health community
**Column 2 — Approach:**
- Build on strengths and improvement opportunities outlined in the 2008 evaluation
- Joint working, collaborative, co-creation — not client–consultant work
- Duration ~22 weeks: 10 weeks diagnosis and design, 12 weeks delivery
- Scope: 5 Partnership bodies — GDF, MDR-TB WG, CFCS, Advocacy, and CM&B teams
- External interactions with other GHPs, e.g., RBM, UNAIDS, GAVI, GF
**Column 3 — 3 main end-products:**
- Successful performance-improvement pilots in selected Partnership bodies, with documentation to support roll-out to other bodies
- A co-authored project report, suitable for publication in major journals, detailing the experience and lessons for other GHPs
- A "practitioner's guide" to support McKinsey teams conducting similar work
_Source: McKinsey analysis._

## framework-processes — content — We think about performance in terms of both processes and enablers — (1) Processes
**Layout:** Numbered six-step process chain across the top (the deck's recurring motif: 1 Set objectives → 2 Set targets → 3 Establish clear metrics → 4 Track and disseminate metrics → 5 Review performance → 6 Celebrate achievements and take further action), labeled "Performance management." Below, three framed description boxes grouping the steps.
**Step group 1–2 — Set objectives & targets:**
- Set time-bound objectives that contribute to fulfilling the mission and vision
- Set metrics that directly measure agreed objectives
- Set additional metrics that cascade down from the key metric
- Set quantitative targets for the metrics; translate these into operational plans and budgets
**Step group 3–4 — Establish & track metrics:**
- Produce progress reports
- Disseminate reports to team members and stakeholders at pre-aligned intervals
**Step group 5–6 — Review & act:**
- Use reports to discuss progress in structured review meetings
- Identify possible performance gaps and decide how to address them
- Celebrate successes and take any required corrective actions
_Source: McKinsey analysis._

## challenges-processes — content — Many GHPs find some performance processes challenging given their complex environment and structure
**Layout:** Numbered six-step process chain across the top (the recurring motif). Below, three framed boxes mapping challenges to the early, middle, and late steps.
**Box 1 — Setting objectives & targets:**
- Getting from a vision to specific objectives for the partnership and its bodies, rather than directly to activities
- Aligning divergent partner views on which objectives to pursue
- Committing to targets is difficult because some targets are not entirely deliverable by the partnership, the voluntary nature of partnerships, and consequences of missing targets (e.g., future funding)
**Box 2 — Metrics & data:**
- Agreeing the right metrics for objectives that are difficult to measure, e.g., awareness about TB
- Aligning organization structures with performance drivers and metrics to enable clear accountability
- Getting good performance data given in-country data-gathering limitations
**Box 3 — Advocacy specifics:**
- Setting advocacy objectives that stay current and relevant in changing external circumstances
- Finding and making visible tactical advocacy opportunities for partners
_Source: Interviews with Stop TB Partnership, GAVI, and UNAIDS._

## challenges-enablers — content — Many GHPs also struggle with the right enablers
**Layout:** 2×2 quadrant grid mirroring the enablers framework (Committed leadership / Capacity / Communications / Culture), each cell stating the challenge for that enabler.
**Quadrant — Committed leadership:** Ensuring leaders regularly lead and participate in performance reviews, in spite of many other calls on their time.
**Quadrant — Capacity:** Allocating appropriate resources to performance management given limited resources for internal processes.
**Quadrant — Communications:** Establishing a shared understanding of accountability across the different backgrounds of partners; keeping partners in a loose working group engaged and motivated.
**Quadrant — Culture:** Moving from a culture of performance measurement (extensive reporting) to performance management (open discussion of outcomes); defining ambitious goals despite a consensus-building culture that tends toward abstract objectives.
_Source: Interviews with Stop TB Partnership, GAVI, and UNAIDS._

## strengths — content — The Stop TB Partnership displays a number of strengths across performance processes
**Layout:** Single framed list of strength statements, each a bold lead-in (the relevant process step) followed by an example; square bullets, generous within-box density.
**Strengths (process):**
- **Setting objectives** — GDF objectives are clearly defined and distinguish the change GDF hopes to bring about in the world (e.g., MDGs — 70% of TB cases diagnosed, 85% cure rate) from the internal goals it sets itself
- **Tracking and disseminating metrics** — despite limited resources, GDF tracks and reports on a wide variety of metrics to meet the different demands of donors
- **Establishing clear metrics and setting targets** — the MDR-TB Working Group defines concrete metrics (e.g., patients with access to MDR-TB treatment) and sets specific targets (e.g., for 2009: 200,000 patients, 4 projects)
- **Reviewing performance** — the Advocacy Team conducts an in-depth review of performance against objectives stated in its funding proposal, taking stock of results and lessons learned
_Source: McKinsey analysis; interviews._

## gdf-8-steps — content — The team followed an eight-step process to create streamlined, structured KPIs, dashboards, and review meetings
**Layout:** 2×4 grid of labeled step cards (a–h), each with a navy letter chip and a short action; even gutters, equal cells.
**Steps:**
- **a** — Collate all GDF metrics reported on into one database: ~250 in total
- **b** — Agree on GDF objectives and cluster metrics/KPIs to objectives
- **c** — Develop a detailed KPI tree for each objective
- **d** — Streamline detailed trees, excluding activity-related or non-GDF-owned KPIs
- **e** — Assign owners to, and define review frequency of, each KPI
- **f** — Develop a clear cascade of KPIs from teams up to the COO annual performance review
- **g** — Create dashboards and review calendars for each team
- **h** — Hold a workshop on conducting performance dialogues
_Source: McKinsey analysis._

## advocacy-3-steps — content — Three steps ensure that emerging opportunities are incorporated into advocacy partners' plans and the Framework Document
**Layout:** Three-step horizontal flow (numbered I → II → III chevrons or arrow-linked cards), each step a navy-headed card with a short tagline and supporting bullets.
**Step I — "Develop the sunflower" — Get more information:**
- Make advocacy every partner's business and increase information sharing
- Extend the reach of TB advocacy beyond the Partnership
**Step II — "Filter the intelligence" — Filter the information received:**
- Set up a process that filters information for validity, impact, global relevance, and feasibility
**Step III — "Keep the framework relevant" — Create or adjust objectives and plans:**
- For tactical opportunities requiring immediate action, create new or adjusted objectives and define what success looks like
- For all other opportunities, draft adjustments into the Framework Document and seek advice from the AAC
_Source: Workshops._

## advocacy-filters — content — Information from an active, extended network is filtered across four criteria
**Layout:** Four-stage horizontal filter funnel — four equal navy-headed cards left to right (Validity → Global → Impact → Feasibility), arrows between; "unfiltered information in" at the left, "filtered intelligence out" at the right.
**Eyebrow:** PRELIMINARY
**Filter — Validity:** The received information is checked for validity with a second source: "Is the information accurate and true?"
**Filter — Global:** The information is filtered for global relevance: "Does it have implications beyond local/national boundaries?"
**Filter — Impact:** Once validated, the information is filtered for work-plan impact: can it affect policy, donors, or public opinion?
**Filter — Feasibility:** Determine whether the information can feasibly and realistically be translated into sufficiently impactful activities.
_Source: Workshops. Apply the filter at the start of advocacy meetings._
